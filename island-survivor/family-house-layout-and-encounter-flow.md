# Extraction: Dead Isles — Family House Layout and Encounter Flow

## purpose
This document defines the family house as the first major human-threat landmark on Home Island. It should act as the bridge between early survival, first structured human combat, engineer rescue, and wider settlement progression.

## design goals
The family house encounter should:
- feel like a major step up from early zombie pressure
- introduce human enemies as a different kind of threat
- reward preparation and cautious approach
- stay readable enough for the first playable
- end with the engineer rescue and clear next-step progression

## narrative role
The family house is where the game shifts from:
- surviving the island
- into confronting organised human danger
- and then into building the first real settlement relationship

It is not just a loot spot. It is a progression landmark.

## encounter role in the opening arc
The intended arc is:
1. survive crash and establish basic camp
2. gather, hunt, cook, and build enough to stabilise
3. meet the first zombies
4. explore further inland
5. discover the family house as a dangerous occupied location
6. prepare and assault or infiltrate the house
7. clear the gang threat
8. rescue the engineer
9. return the engineer to camp
10. begin specialist settlement progression

## location identity
The family house should feel:
- residential
- once safe and normal
- now tense and occupied
- dangerous because people have taken it over
- emotionally stronger than normal Home Island spaces

## recommended location structure
The family house area should include:
- approach path through light exterior terrain
- visible outer area or yard
- one or two exterior entry options
- main house interior
- side rooms or bedrooms
- kitchen/common room
- gang hold area
- hostage or locked holding space for the engineer
- stash area

This layout gives the player readable choices without requiring a huge level.

## encounter pacing goals
The encounter should have three phases:
1. approach and read the situation
2. breach and clear the occupied house
3. secure the engineer and finish the site

## phase 1 — approach and scouting
### purpose
Let the player understand that this is not just another infected building.

### desired player observations
- signs of occupation
- gang presence
- patrol or lookout behaviour
- possible entry routes
- risk of direct frontal attack

### environmental signals
Use things like:
- voices
- light movement
- makeshift barricades
- scavenged gear
- bodies, mess, or camp signs
- blocked windows or doors

### design goal
The player should feel encouraged to stop, watch, and plan.

## phase 2 — breach and combat
### purpose
This is the first structured human-combat landmark in the game.

### intended encounter identity
- tense but manageable
- close-quarters and dangerous
- more deliberate than zombie fights
- readable enough for first playable

### recommended gang composition
Use the previously defined structure:
- 2 scavenger melee
- 1 scavenger ranged or support threat
- 1 bruiser
- 1 gang leader

Optional:
- one extra basic melee enemy if balancing requires it

### combat flow
The fight should be able to unfold in a few possible ways:
- stealthy outer pick-off then house breach
- direct aggressive assault
- cautious room-by-room push
- baiting enemies outside where possible

### key rule
Do not force only one “correct” method.

## phase 3 — engineer rescue and secure aftermath
### purpose
Turn the encounter from combat success into progression success.

### expected result
After the fight:
- the house is effectively cleared
- the engineer is found and confirmed safe
- the player understands this rescue matters
- the next camp progression step is obvious

### rescue implementation rule
Do not rely on complex escort AI in the first implementation.

### recommended first-pass solution
Use rescue confirmation plus safe transition logic.
For example:
- player reaches engineer room
- engineer interaction completes rescue state
- brief dialogue or acknowledgement occurs
- engineer is considered returned via a clean transition or follow-up home-state update

This keeps the moment reliable.

## entry route design
The family house should support multiple approach styles without becoming a giant sandbox.

### good approach options
- front door approach
- side entry or broken access point
- rear access or window-side route

### rule
These do not need fully different level routes, just enough variation to support player choice.

## room function suggestions
### exterior yard or front area
- first line of gang presence
- possible lookout or melee patrol

### kitchen/common room
- shared occupied space
- food and domestic loot identity
- possible combat pivot room

### bedroom or side rooms
- close-quarters combat
- domestic story items
- possible stash and clue locations

### gang stash room
- concentrated reward space
- meds, carry gear, stash identity

### hostage room / engineer hold space
- emotionally important endpoint
- should feel more controlled and protected

## enemy distribution suggestions
### outer area
- 1 basic melee or lookout-style enemy

### common room / circulation space
- 1 or 2 standard gang enemies

### interior choke point
- bruiser or stronger close-range pressure

### engineer-adjacent space
- gang leader or strongest defender

This makes progression through the house feel structured.

## loot identity in the encounter
The family house should reward the player for taking the risk.

### strongest reward types
- better backpack or carry upgrade
- meds
- gang stash items
- family photo or emotional domestic item
- engineer-related clue or progression item

### rule
The family house should feel rewarding, but it must not undermine later progression with random firearm abundance.

## emotional tone
This location should carry more emotional weight than a normal combat space.

### why
It is:
- domestic
- occupied by hostile humans
- tied to the first rescued specialist
- one of the first places where the world feels broken in a personal way

### supporting detail ideas
- family remnants
- signs of forced occupation
- improvised gang living conditions
- restrained or isolated engineer space

## difficulty target
The family house should feel like:
- the first real combat checkpoint
- dangerous if rushed
- beatable with preparation
- a clear step up from early zombie encounters

### avoid
- making it feel impossible if the player arrives a little undergeared
- making it trivial if the player only has primitive tools
- turning it into a full tactical shooter mission

## player preparation expectation
Before attempting the family house, the player should ideally have:
- primitive melee weapon
- bow if available
- basic healing supplies
- some cooked food and water
- at least a small amount of confidence in combat controls

This makes the encounter feel earned rather than random.

## aftermath and transition
After completing the family house:
- the player should understand the engineer is now the first major rescued NPC
- the next step should clearly become bringing him into the camp progression
- the house should remain a cleared landmark and a memory point on Home Island

## summary
The family house should serve as:
- the first real human-combat landmark
- the bridge from survival into settlement progression
- the gateway to the engineer rescue chain
- a rewarding but tense domestic combat location

It is one of the most important first-playable encounters and should be kept focused, readable, and memorable.
