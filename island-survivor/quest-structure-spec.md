# Extraction: Dead Isles — Quest Structure Spec

## purpose
This document defines the quest structure for the game, including quest categories, delivery style, reward logic, guidance rules, and how quests support settlement growth and island progression.

## design goals
Quests should:
1. feel grounded in survival and the world
2. unlock real progression
3. make rescued NPCs matter
4. guide island progression without over-railroading
5. support story without becoming cutscene-heavy or checklist-driven

Quests should feel like helping real people and pushing the settlement forward.

## core quest categories
The game should use these quest types:
1. bring items
2. rescue survivor
3. clear location
4. map or clue recovery
5. boss kill
6. build hut or station
7. collect samples or materials
8. delivery or return trip

## bring item quests
### purpose
Used for practical survivor support and trust building.

### good examples
- bring food
- bring water
- bring bandages
- bring tools
- bring ore samples
- bring documents

### usage rule
Use these when they make narrative and systems sense. Do not let them become repetitive filler.

## rescue survivor quests
### purpose
These are core progression quests that grow the settlement, unlock specialists, and expand progression paths.

### example roles
- engineer
- builder
- medic
- technician
- child survivor
- player's own child later

### rule
A rescued survivor should visibly change the world and the camp.

## clear location quests
### purpose
Focused combat objectives tied to practical outcomes.

### examples
- clear the family house
- clear a mine section
- clear a route or building to make access possible

### rule
A clear quest should usually support rescue, access, recovery, or progression, not just enemy count removal.

## map or clue recovery quests
### purpose
These drive island progression and mystery.

### examples
- recover route map
- recover mine documents
- locate hotel clue
- find evidence pointing to another island

### placement rule
Important clues should be semi-random among valid locations, never fully random.

## boss kill quests
### purpose
Used sparingly for milestone moments.

### examples
- Mining Island boss
- later island apex infected
- later major human threat leaders

### rule
Boss kills should unlock real change such as access, trust, clue progress, or settlement growth.

## build hut or station quests
### purpose
Tie progression directly into Home Island growth.

### examples
- build engineer hut
- build furnace
- build workbench
- build later specialist shelter

### rule
These should reinforce camp growth and unlock practical new capability.

## collect samples or materials quests
### purpose
Support revisits and specialist-driven progression.

### examples
- bring coal
- bring iron ore
- bring infected residue samples
- bring machine parts
- recover technical material

### rule
These should fit the specialist role asking for them.

## delivery or return-trip quests
### purpose
Support repeated island runs and continuity.

### examples
- carry tools back to camp
- return with documents
- bring supplies to support an NPC chain

### rule
These should support rhythm and continuity, not feel like chore work.

## quest delivery style
Best delivery methods:
- NPC conversation
- clue or item discovery
- world event discovery
- returning home after major progress
- camp dialogue prompting next steps

Quest delivery should feel practical and world-driven, not like giant glowing notice boards.

## guidance rules
Guidance should vary by quest type.

### full markers
Use for:
- build hut
- deliver supplies
- return to known NPC
- clearly known objective locations

### area markers
Use for:
- search a zone
- recover a clue from a site
- find an object in a rough region

### low-guidance or clue-based
Use for:
- optional discoveries
- story traces
- some family-search beats
- mystery-heavy content

## reward types
Quest rewards should mainly be practical progression rewards:
- new island unlocked
- new NPC service unlocked
- new recipe or station unlocked
- new clue or map
- item access
- settlement growth
- trust progression

Avoid abstract XP-heavy reward design as the primary motivator.

## quest chain pattern
A strong quest chain usually follows this shape:
1. discover problem
2. do practical first step
3. stabilise situation
4. gain trust or access
5. unlock area, service, or person
6. receive follow-up goals

## engineer chain as model
1. discover engineer
2. clear family house
3. rescue engineer
4. build engineer hut
5. bring food, water, and bandage
6. bring work materials
7. unlock Mining Island
8. bring back mining outputs
9. unlock pistol and bullet support

## settlement-driven philosophy
Good quests should improve at least one of these:
- survival
- capability
- reach
- knowledge
- emotional investment

Every strong quest should make the camp feel safer, more functional, more populated, or more hopeful.

## story integration rule
Story should be carried mainly by:
- survivor personalities
- location clues
- what progression unlocks mean
- the changing settlement
- gradual family-search reveals

Avoid giant exposition dumps.

## avoid
- repetitive checklist filler
- kill-X quest spam
- too many simultaneous quest logs early
- giant map-marker overload
- rewards that are only abstract numbers
- NPCs who stop mattering after one unlock

## first-playable quest priorities
The first playable quest structure mainly needs to support:
1. opening survival sequence
2. family house assault
3. engineer rescue
4. engineer hut and support chain
5. Mining Island unlock
6. Mining Island return progression
7. firearm progression through engineer
